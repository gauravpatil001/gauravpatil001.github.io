I scored 331 on the GRE in my first attempt: 165 in Verbal Reasoning and 166 in Quantitative Reasoning. That score played an important role in helping me receive a fellowship and scholarship from Texas A&M University, even though I applied five months after the financial-aid deadlines.

I began preparing during the last week of January 2020, studied for about six weeks, and took the test in the first week of March. This post outlines the week-by-week strategy I followed. It assumes that you have at least six weeks to prepare and can study for approximately four hours each day.

> **A note on the test format:** The GRE has changed since I took it in 2020. The format described below reflects my experience at that time. Before planning your preparation, check the official ETS website for the current test structure, timing, scoring, and policies.

## Understand the exam first

The GRE, or Graduate Record Examination, is a standardized test administered by ETS. It is used by graduate and business schools to evaluate applicants from a wide range of educational and cultural backgrounds. It measures verbal reasoning, quantitative reasoning, critical thinking, and analytical writing.

When I took the exam, it lasted about four hours and included six sections, with a ten-minute break after the third section. Analytical Writing always came first and consisted of two essays: Analyze an Issue and Analyze an Argument. Each essay had a 30-minute time limit.

The remaining sections included two Verbal Reasoning sections, two Quantitative Reasoning sections, and one unscored section that could be either verbal or quantitative. The unscored section was not identified, so every section had to be treated as though it counted.

The GRE was section-adaptive: performance in the first verbal or quantitative section influenced the difficulty of the next section in that subject. Verbal and quantitative scores ranged from 130 to 170 in one-point increments, while Analytical Writing was scored from 0 to 6 in half-point increments. Universities generally considered the section scores individually, not just the combined total. GRE scores were valid for five years.

## The resources I used

My preparation relied on the following resources:

- The three official ETS books: *The Official Guide to the GRE General Test*, *Official GRE Verbal Reasoning Practice Questions*, and *Official GRE Quantitative Reasoning Practice Questions*
- Manhattan Prep's Essential and Advanced GRE vocabulary flash cards
- Manhattan Prep's *5 lb. Book of GRE Practice Problems*
- *Word Power Made Easy* by Norman Lewis
- A Magoosh premium plan

I also used a quantitative aptitude book intended for the Common Admission Test (CAT) and watched YouTube videos when I needed to review mathematical concepts.

## Week 1: Build a foundation with official material

Start with the official ETS books. Read them carefully, solve every question, and study every provided solution. Because these books contain authentic GRE material, they give you the clearest picture of the questions you are likely to encounter.

The practice tests will establish your starting point and show you how much improvement you need.

As you read, write down every unfamiliar or difficult word. Look up all its meanings, not only the most common one. The GRE often tests secondary or less familiar meanings. Read example sentences as well so that you understand how the word works in context. This lets you approach vocabulary from several angles at once.

## Weeks 2 and 3: Practice systematically

Next, take on Manhattan Prep's *5 lb. Book of GRE Practice Problems*. Its size can be intimidating, but the best response is simply to begin. The goal is not merely to complete a large number of questions; it is to learn the correct approach to each type of problem. Allow about two weeks to work through the book.

At the same time, begin studying the Manhattan Prep vocabulary flash cards. The Essential and Advanced decks contain 500 words each. Start with the Essential deck.

Each card includes a word, its meaning, example sentences, synonyms, antonyms, usage guidance, origins, or other helpful details. Taken together, this information helps you become genuinely familiar with a word instead of memorizing a single definition.

### My flash-card method

The cards are arranged alphabetically, but I did not find alphabetical memorization effective for learning a thousand difficult words. I found it easier to remember words when I could connect them through synonyms, antonyms, or shared connotations.

During the first pass, I sorted the cards into thematic stacks. One stack covered emotions; another covered quantity. Any word even loosely related to a theme went into the corresponding stack. I eventually created about eight to ten stacks and distributed all 500 Essential cards among them.

I studied each stack as follows:

1. Shuffle the stack.
2. Read the first card and study its definition and example.
3. Place it at the back of the stack, then continue through the remaining cards.
4. When the first card returns to the top, try to recall its meaning.
5. If you remember it, remove it from the stack. If you do not, place it at the back again.
6. Repeat until you have correctly recalled and removed every card.

This method creates connections among related words and improves recall. Follow the same process with the Advanced deck, and review the cards daily until the exam. Each successive review will become faster because more of the words will already be familiar.

## Week 4: Strengthen vocabulary and quantitative fluency

In the fourth week, work through *Word Power Made Easy* by Norman Lewis. It is a dense book filled with exercises and explanations, but its etymological approach, which involves learning words through their origins, can significantly improve your command of English.

That matters because the Verbal Reasoning section is demanding. Spending too long trying to interpret a single unfamiliar word creates time pressure, while attempting a question without understanding a key word can lead to avoidable mistakes. If you view GRE preparation as an opportunity to improve your English more broadly, the time invested in this book is worthwhile.

Continue quantitative preparation during this week as well. Review formulas, practice mental arithmetic, reduce your reliance on rough calculations, and avoid reaching for the on-screen calculator when a calculation can be completed quickly without it.

## Week 5 onward: Move from paper to screen

Until this stage, most of the preparation has taken place in books. The GRE, however, is computer-based, and reading and solving problems on a screen feels different from working on paper. You must become comfortable reading digital content while quickly noting the important information.

I used Magoosh to make that transition. At the time, it offered approximately 1,200 GRE-style questions, each with a detailed written solution and a video explaining the appropriate approach. I recommend solving the full question bank if your schedule permits.

This is also the time to focus seriously on time management. Work under timed conditions and complete all available practice tests.

### Prepare for Analytical Writing

By this point, I was ready to focus on Analytical Writing. I followed the advice in the official guides, the Manhattan Prep book, and Magoosh.

My approach was to aim for at least six paragraphs per essay:

- A clear introduction showing that I understood the prompt
- At least three relevant arguments
- One counterargument
- A constructive way forward
- A conclusion that tied the response together

Essay requirements have since changed, so adapt this advice to the current GRE format. Whatever the prompt, the essential skill is the same: organize your reasoning clearly and support it with relevant arguments.

Keep reviewing your vocabulary flash cards every day.

## The final week: Use official mock tests

During the last week, I completed the two official ETS mock tests available to GRE test-takers. In my experience, their scores were close to the score I ultimately received on the actual exam.

Use these tests to identify any remaining weaknesses. If your score is not yet where it needs to be, revise, practice more questions, and consider postponing the exam if that is practical. It is better to address persistent mistakes than to walk into the test hoping they will disappear.

## Final thoughts

GRE preparation can be expensive. The registration fee and study resources add up quickly. However, earning a strong score on the first attempt can bring meaningful benefits. A high score may strengthen applications to competitive universities and help with consideration for financial aid. Because scores remain valid for several years, a strong result may also support future plans for a master's degree, PhD, or MBA.

No study plan can guarantee a particular result, but completing these resources carefully and learning from every mistake put me in a position to score above 330. Practice consistently, treat solutions as lessons rather than answer keys, and go into the exam with confidence.

Best of luck!
